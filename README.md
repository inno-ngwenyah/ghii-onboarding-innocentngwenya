GHII Key Stakeholders and the MEDU Project
-------------------------------------------
The Global Health Informatics Institute (GHII) collaborates with a diverse group of stakeholders to strengthen health information systems and promote data-driven decision-making in Malawi. Its key stakeholders include the Ministry of Health (MOH), which provides national leadership, policy direction, and oversight for digital health initiatives. Healthcare workers—such as clinicians, data clerks, and facility managers, play a critical role in generating and using health data at facility level. Development partners and donors, including organizations such as PEPFAR and Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), provide funding and technical support. Additional stakeholders include implementing partners (IPs e.g., EGPAF, PIH, Lighthouse, Beira, Pakachere, etc.), academic institutions, and district health management teams, all of whom contribute to system strengthening and data use. Ultimately, communities are the end beneficiaries through improved healthcare delivery and outcomes.

The Malawi EMR Data Use (MEDU) project—implemented by GHII, focuses on improving the use of electronic medical record (EMR) data, particularly for HIV programs. MEDU, now in its fourth year, aims to centralize HIV data, strengthen Data Quality Assessment (DQA), and enhance the use of data from the Central Data Repository (CDR). The current phase of the project emphasizes broadening the use of CDR data and data use tools through active stakeholder engagement at both national and district levels.

A core objective of MEDU is improving the quality of CDR data. To achieve this, the project implements multiple data quality checks that assess completeness, plausibility, and conformance. These checks are applied both before data is uploaded to the CDR and after ingestion, ensuring that only reliable data is used for analysis and reporting.

The project also promotes the use of CDR data for routine reporting. These reports are broadly categorized into two groups. The first includes donor-driven reports aligned with PEPFAR/CDC requirements, such as TX_NEW, TX_CURR, TX_CURR MMD, TX_ML, TX_RTT, and TX_PVLS, which are critical for monitoring HIV program performance. The second category includes MOH-focused reports, such as cohort analyses and survival analysis, which support national planning and clinical decision-making.

Another key focus area is improving accessibility and use of data. MEDU supports the development of data visualization tools, dashboards, and routine data review processes that enable stakeholders at facility, district, and national levels to interpret and act on data effectively. These efforts help transform raw data into actionable insights.

Finally, MEDU works toward increasing the capacity of the MOH to manage and use CDR data. This includes training and mentorship for MOH staff on data analysis and use, as well as strengthening systems for data access and approval processes. By building local capacity, the project ensures sustainability and ownership of data systems.

In summary, through collaboration with its stakeholders, GHII’s MEDU project seeks to transform centralized HIV data into a strategic asset for improving patient care, enhancing program performance, and strengthening Malawi’s overall health system.
